Problem

Massachusetts has one of the least accountable state legislatures in the US, leading to a wide gap between voters’ beliefs & the legislature’s priorities. Act on Mass activates grassroots organizers & voters to hold the state legislature accountable to passing a progressive agenda. Act on Mass needed a website to further their reach. Their old website lacked user engagement features.

Solution

The redesigned Actonmass.org aims to create an intuitive experience for users who need information about current progressive bills, need to contact their state legislators, or would like to join volunteer efforts with Act on Mass.

Discovery & Research

Comparitive Analysis

Finding a Niche

The goal of the competitive analysis was to study other nonprofit political advocacy organizations, on the state and federal level, and understand how they educate their users and motivate them to participate. I discovered there was not a product on the market that closed the loop from discovery - education - contact elected official.

Features

There was a legislature search on both of the state level competitors websites. So we knew we needed a search and a summary bio page for each elected official at the state level. In terms of bill education the product on the market resembles a list form of bills and small summaries. All of the competitors offered blog features and regular updates. These findings helped shape the MVP and clarify where Act on Mass fit into the market.

Survey 1

The goal of this research was to find out:

  • 1. Sticking points between voters and elected officials
  • 2. If voters cared about transparency
  • 3. If voters wanted bill education

From this survey we learned that voters were hungry for more information about current bills. We also learned that these voters would contact their elected officials and contact them regularly. This reinforced Act on Mass purpose as a non-profit organization. This survey didn’t give us a complete picture of what the roadblocks were between voters and officials. So we sent out a second survey to get a more robust picture of what these voters' stories were.



view user research

Survey 2

Now we had to figure out how to close the loop between voters and elected officials. We sent this survey to the survey 1 participants who left their email for follow up. The goal of this research was to find out:

  • 1. What are sticking points between voters and legislators communicating
  • 2. What issues are the most important to voters

We discovered that the voters would attempt to contact their legislators. However, many voters reported challenges getting in touch, feeling it was pointless, and not feeling prepared. This survey showed us how important bill education is to voters. Also, providing tools that help voters prepare for conversations with legislators is a high priority.

Personas

After analyzing the user surveys we wanted to take the research one step farther and create user personas. AOM users are diverse in age. We wanted to make sure we were designing for the targeted users. The stakeholders and myself are tech-savvy and fall into a younger age group. We didn’t want to let our bias overtake the project. We wanted to make sure we were understanding the targested users motivations and goals. We were working with a tight budget so we opted for lightweight user personas.

Testing & Prototyping

Mockups

After I finished the initial wireframes I moved onto prototyping and testing. We were on a tight timeline so I created the first round mockups within a week. Next, I moved onto rapid testing and iterating the designs. Since I was the only designer on the project I needed regular feedback from users.

Testing Round 1 - Moderated & In-Person

The first round of testing was facilitated at the Act on Mass office. We had Act on Mass users come to test the prototype. Seven users tested the site. I read them a testing script and guided them through the Invision Prototype. We gathered a lot of information during these testing sessions. To briefly illustrate a few key changes I've displayed the home page.

  • 1. The users did not like the videos in the hero section.
  • 2. The users did not like the email collection at the top of the webpage.
  • 3. Some of the testers stated that they rarely scroll down on webpages.
  • 4. The colors of the website did not match the users idea of AOM personality. Their online presence is humorous and at time snarky.
  • 1. We changed the hero to feature an image instead of a video. Users requested the website humanize Act on Mass. We did this by adding more image of activists in action.
  • 2. We moved the email capture below the hero. We also seperated the call to actions.
  • 3. We trimmed the hompage content. We only left key features.
  • 4. We added a electric blue to the color palette to energize to the personality of the branding.
Testing Round 2 - Unmoderated & Remote

After finishing version 2 of the mockups I needed to conduct another round of user testing to test the designs. We were still on a tight timeline so we opted to do unmoderated, remote user testing via Maze. We sent the Maze test to an Act on Mass email list. We received 21 results. This allowed us to gain a deeper understanding through combining both qualitative and quantitative information.



  • 1. This testing block asked users to navigate to the Safe Communities Act. The test started on the immigration page where the bill lives.
  • 2. Users were trying to exit the page to navigate elsewhere to find the bill.
  • 1. To solve this problem I added a new page that shows all of the bills that are available to browse. This is included under "learn more".
  • 2. I also took steps to guide the users eyes towards the bill section. I made muted the hero section colors.
  • 3. I also added more white space around the bill section.
  • 4. I increased the size of the bill headings and decreased the weight of the bullet points to increase readability.
  • 5. I also increased the size of the section heading.
Testing Round 3 - Moderated & In-person

After I made revisions based on the Maze testing I moved into coding a live prototype. There were some limitations to testing within InVision and I wanted to do additional testing to make sure we solved the problems highlighted earlier. After coding the prototype I conducted 5 moderated testing sessions. They were a mix of in-person and remote. I tested the same issues as the prior Maze testing. The problems areas were testing much better and had less sticking points. However, I discovered a few more sticking points that could be improved.



  • 1. I asked the users to navigate to the "immigration" issue.
  • 2. When users started to search for the "immigration issue" they would hover around the "examples of state house failures."
  • 1. To solve this problem I removed the "examples of state house failures" section. It was not essential to the website and did not have any CTA.
  • 2. This allowed me to move up the "key progressive issues" section, which is central to the website.

Conclusion

Lessons Learned

This was an exciting opportunity to work with a client to develop a solution to help voters contact their legislators. If I had restarted this project I would:

  • • Have even more meetings with the stakeholders. Communication was key to keep the project moving in a good direction.

  • • I would have estimated the timeline better. Completing a project this big (including frontend development) is a lot to do in 3 months.

  • • I would do more testing in the wireframe phase to save time during prototype testing.
