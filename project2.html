Introduction

I met with Erika and Matt to volunteer my services to help redesign their non-profit website. I had some time on my hands and they were a nonprofit seeking volunteers. Every time we met, I learned something new about Massachusetts’ legislative and political processes, and how one influences the other.

For example, did you know that in MA, committee votes & testimony are not public? We elect our representatives based on how well their campaign platforms align with our interests, but after the election, we cannot track their voting records.

As a junior designer, I was eager to apply my newly-learned skills! However, I could not foresee how complex this website redesign was going to be..

Problem

Act on Mass has identified a breakdown in communication between voters and their elected representatives. This creates a significant transparency issue.

Erika, Matt and I met in their Boston office to explore possible solutions to this problem. I listened to them talk and quickly scribbled notes. I began to see just how little I, and likely other voters, knew about how a bill does (or does not) become a law in my own state!

Erika travels around the state, firing up voters to demand legislative transparency. Erika’s presentations are effective, but this approach is not scalable or sustainable.

We decided Act on Mass needed to equip voters with tools to enable legislative transparency. It also needed to provide means for the public to engage with their legislators, to educate themselves on the legislative process and to monitor the progress of current bills. In short, a way to hold their elected officials accountable to the people.

Discovery & Research

Comparitive Analysis

Transparency in government is a popular issue and I knew there had to be other organizations trying to solve this same problem. I studied how other non-profits educated their users and motivated them to participate. The competitor’s I studied did not emphasize bills in session as a learning tool for the voter. However, it was helpful to understand the environment in which Act on Mass competes.

Survey 1

I decided to check in with MA voters and get a sense of their priorities. I created a survey in Google Forms and distributed the survey to the Act on Mass email list. We also posted the survey on social media. A few re-tweets later and we collected 128 survey responses in 72 hours!

The goals of this survey:

  • 1. Do voters want more information about bills?
  • 2. Do voters care about transparency in government?
  • 3. What are the sticking points between voters and legislators communicating?

We were encouraged by how many participants wanted more bill education. The survey was informative but it did not uncover the communication obstacles between voters and legislators.



view user research

Survey 2

Erika and Matt agreed that I could send a follow up survey to participants who left their emails and who opted in to answer further questions. I developed a brief follow-up survey using open-ended questions and distributed it using Google Forms.

This was a great opportunity to gather insight from voters!

The goals of this survey:

  • 1. What are the obstacles between voters and legislators communicating?
  • 2. What political issues are most important to voters?

We received 19 responses from the second survey. The responses characterized voters who felt powerless and overwhelmed when trying to follow the state house. Voters did not feel prepared to argue bill specifics with their legislators. This was an important turning point in our discovery. Voters wanted more than scripts to read when they got in touch with legislators. Voters wanted to understand the complexity of bills. They wanted to deliver compelling arguments.

Personas

In order to capture the full characterization of Act on Mass users I created personas. Personas are fictional characters based on the user research. Their purpose is to create a reliable representation of a key audience. AOM users are diverse in age. We wanted to make sure we were designing for their targeted users. Erika, Matt and I are tech-savvy and fall into a younger age group. We did not want to let our bias overtake the project. We were working with a tight timeline so we opted for lightweight user personas.

Information Architecture

User Stories

Next, I needed to distill the information in order to clarify the goals. A website redesign can quickly become overwhelming and scattered. As a designer I knew I needed to set achievable goals. Erika and Matt had a wishlist of features they wanted to include in the website redesign. We needed to agree on a Minimum Viable Product (the bare minimum features to have a working product or to meet the stated objectives.)

The user surveys painted a clear picture that citizens wanted more bill education and a means to get in contact with their elected officials. Bill education became our focus. I listed out all of the user stories and assigned each a priority level from low to high. I then met with Erika and Matt to clarify the direction of the MVP.

User Flows

Once Erika and Matt had agreed that bill education was our focus, we mapped out where pages would live on the website. Erika and Matt chose which progressive issues they wanted to highlight. Each issue had 1 - 3 supporting bills. We started with nine progressive issues which meant we would have between 9 to 27 web pages dedicated to bills. Each bill has a unique story. We discussed ideas for how to consolidate the legislative process into a visual story for each bill.

Wireframes

The bill education feature was not a redesign but a new feature. We used the user flows as a guide to frame the sketches. We were working in tandem with the software engineers to try and understand what was going to be possible. Erika, Matt and I were trying to synthesize all their knowledge about bills into a consistent layout that would effectively tell a story for a bill.

We started this process by having a brainstorm and sketch session. Erika had some key parts of her presentation that she knew resonated with voters. We started with those features and sketched out several potential scenarios. The software engineer was working in another room. We would pull him in anytime we had a question about the engineering capabilities.

Testing & Prototyping

Mockups

After I finished the initial wireframes I moved onto prototyping and testing. We were on a tight timeline so I created the first round mockups within a week. Next, I moved onto rapid testing and iterating the designs. Since I was the only designer on the project I needed regular feedback from users.

Testing Round 1 - Moderated & In-Person

The first round of testing was facilitated at the Act on Mass office. We had Act on Mass users come to test the prototype. Seven users tested the site. I read them a testing script and guided them through the Invision Prototype. We gathered a lot of information during these testing sessions. To briefly illustrate a few key changes I've displayed the hero section of the bill page.

Testing Round 2 - Unmoderated & Remote

The first round of testing encouraged a lot of revisions. After making changes to the new prototypes I needed to test them again. The main objective of this testing was to make sure that users could navigate to issues and bills easily. The test was unmoderated, remote testing via Maze. There were 21 participants. I opted for this testing method so we could move the project forward in a timely fashion.



Testing Round 3 - Moderated & In-person

After I made revisions based on the Maze testing I moved into coding a live prototype. There were some limitations to testing within InVision and I wanted to do additional testing to make sure we solved the problems highlighted earlier. After coding the prototype I conducted 5 moderated testing sessions. They were a mix of in-person and remote. I tested the same issues as the prior Maze testing. The problems areas were testing much better and had less sticking points. However, I discovered a sticking point that could be improved.



Conclusion

Results

I finished the front-end development and handed it off to the software engineers. We are now awaiting for these volunteer engineers to finish the backend. We are all excited for the release of the new website!

What is next?

After we launch this redesign I plan to observe how users are interacting with the new tools. We already have a list of features for version 2.0! We hope to expand on community features, interaction features, and update the blog. The rest of 2020 we will focus on improving the bill page feature. Act on Mass is an all-volunteer project and has a lot of activists who are providing us with feedback as soon as we launch.

What would I do differently?

I would push back on creating mockups until we have tested the wireframes more extensively. I am constantly learning the importance of information architecture and wireframes. Spending more time testing wireframes would have prevented a lot of re-work for me later.
